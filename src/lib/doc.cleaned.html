It is 5:30 a.m. on a cold January day in Las Cruces, New Mexico.

While bird monitoring technicians Nick Schleissman and Grace [Last Name], ease into the morning with a cup of coffee, the rest of the Bird Conservancy of the Rockies crew bustles around to prepare to leave for their ninth consecutive day on the job.

Schleissman, who has worked more field jobs than he can remember, said compared to the spring and summer months during the breeding season, waking up at 5 a.m. is considered “sleeping in” for his line of work.

“As soon as my alarm is going off before 3 a.m., that's it — I'm not doing it,” he said.

The group heads out around 6 a.m. for the Chihuahuan Desert grasslands about an hour north of Las Cruces. As they arrive at their work site, the sun has just started to break over the horizon.

Today, the group is looking for Chestnut-collared Longspur and Baird’s Sparrow — two key grassland bird species facing significant declines in their populations. Their goal is to safely capture, tag and release the target species in order to collect data that can be used for continued monitoring.

“We’re hoping to use the data that we collect here to inform bird conservation work because we’re trying to understand how populations are linked across the annual cycle,” Bird Conservancy of the Rockies research scientist Jessie Reese said.

“That [data] allows us to understand what pressures those birds are facing across different population segments and allows us to understand if things that are happening down here in New Mexico are having an undue influence on any one breeding population and vice versa.”

The findings set off a state of emergency throughout the conservation world, as scientists, policy makers and bird lovers started racing to figure out how to slow down an impending extinction crisis.

In the Great Plains, birds were disappearing at a rate higher than species in any other range, having lost more than half of their populations in the last 50 years.  

Six years later, the same remains true. Despite several efforts to curb losses, grassland birds continue to be the most imperiled species in North America, declining at a rate of 43 percent with some species like the Mountain Plover, Chestnut-collared Longspur and Baird’s Sparrow declining over 67 percent according to the 2025 State of the Birds report.

The main driver behind the widespread decline of birds in the heartland of America is habitat loss.

Despite being among the most endangered ecosystems in the U.S., grasslands are the least protected. According to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, 62 percent of the North American grassland biome has been lost as a result of agricultural conversion, development and the spread of invasive trees — resulting in hundreds of millions of acres of habitat loss for birds. [VISUAL: Habitat Loss Maps]

It is estimated that the Great Plains region loses 1 to 2 million acres of grasslands each year.

[TK: where are grasslands being lost more rapidly?]

A significant majority of birds that rely solely on grasslands for breeding are migratory, traveling long distances between their breeding and wintering grounds each year. Grasslands continue to shrink not only in places critical for breeding, but also in places needed to rest and refuel during migration and wintering months, further exacerbating bird decline.  

“The reality is that we can't restore all of the grassland that's been lost in North America,” Reese said. “We really need to understand where we should be doing conservation work — are there places that are going to be more beneficial where we'll get a bigger bang for our conservation buck?”

Habitat loss is not the only pressure impacting grassland bird populations. Climate change, pesticide usage and woody plant encroachment have also contributed to widespread declines. However, behind all of these threats is one common denominator: humans.

Importance

Without major intervention, several grassland bird species are on track to go extinct [by the end of the century].

Catastrophic losses in bird populations would dramatically alter and threaten the health of the Great Plains region, as birds provide vital ecological services to grasslands, such as pest control and seed dispersal.

As climate change triggers rising temperatures, shifts in precipitation patterns and more frequent extreme weather events such as drought and wildfires, loss of both birds and grasslands will continue to accelerate.

The implications of grassland loss extends further than just birds. With one-fourth of all U.S. crops produced in the Great Plains, declines in grassland health could mean large-scale disruptions in the country’s food supply and economy.

“Grasslands are the lifeblood of the central part of the U.S. and if they disappear, then this landscape will be drastically altered.” Brandt Ryder, chief conservation scientist at Bird Conservancy of the Rockies, said. “You can think of it like a big, giant Dust Bowl.”

Action Pt. 1

Bird Conservancy of the Rockies has been working in grassland bird conservation for a number of years. In 2020, they began working with the Motus program to track birds across their full annual cycle.  

The Motus Wildlife Tracking System is a global collaborative network of automated radio telemetry stations that allows researchers to study the movements of small animals tagged with radio transmitters. After a bird is tagged, station antennas will detect and record when the bird passes by, uploading that detection to an online database thereafter.

The network is made up of over 1,000 stations operated by nearly 900 different organizations across the United States and Canada, allowing for highly collaborative research and data sharing.

Prior to 2020, there was a large lack of Motus stations in the Great Plains region. Researchers knew little about grassland bird long distance movements, migratory timing, stopover locations or survival along the Central Flyway. This knowledge gap posed problems for conservation organizations when trying to create effective conservation strategies and practices for declining species.

“Bird Conservancy has really helped the Motus Network grow in the Great Plains and the Chihuahuan Desert,” Reese said. In the past five years, the organization has installed over 100 radio towers and tagged almost 400 grassland birds.

In doing so, the organization has begun to address that knowledge gap and identify crucial locations for conservation.

Some of the key species that Bird Conservancy targets for Motus tagging are the Baird’s Sparrow, Sprague’s Pipit, Thick-Billed Longspur, Grasshopper Sparrow and Chestnut-collared Longspur.

This is a Chestnut-collared Longspur.

After successfully catching the bird, the Bird Conservancy team records data on its age, sex, weight, muscle and fat content, wings, tail and feathers.

According to the North American Breeding Bird Survey, it is estimated that the Chestnut-collared Longspur population has declined 73 percent since 1967. Degradation and loss of breeding habitat has caused the species to disappear from much of its historical range.

This Chestnut-collared Longspur is a young adult male, which the team was able to determine by examining the feather colors.

Once data on physical measurements is collected, the technicians attach a Motus tag that sits like a tiny, lightweight backpack between the bird’s wings.

Tagged and ready for monitoring, the bird is then safely released back into its habitat.

While the Bird Conservancy research team has specific target species in mind for each day’s work and location, sometimes spontaneity plays a role.

[VISUAL: Footage/Photos of the Lapland Longspur being tagged and released.]

This is a Lapland Longspur.

Lapland Longspurs migrate all throughout North America, stretching as far north as Alaska where they can be found breeding in the arctic tundra. While their remote range has made them very difficult to track, it is believed that their populations have remained mostly stable over the last half century.

It was not one of the group’s target species, so they did not tag it. However, because it was beyond its expected wintering range, the research team still recorded data on the physical measurements of the bird to document the sighting.

“When we got that Lapland Longspur, someone said that's the first county record and we were all really excited about it,” Schleissman said. “That was a little tiny moment where the birder in all of us came out because that wasn't one of our focal species — we were not expecting to get it.”

Who Cares?

Restoring bird populations has wide ranging benefits that go beyond simply improving habitat health and the birds themselves. Birding is a big business.

According to the 2022 National Survey of Fishing, Hunting and Wildlife-Associated Recreation, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service estimated that bird-related activities generated almost $280 billion to the American economy and created 1.4 million jobs.

The survey also estimated that nearly 100 million Americans are bird watchers.

Chris Chappell became interested in birds at just 8 years old after he took care of a couple of chicks that fell out of a nest in his backyard.

A few years later, on a vacation with his family in Arizona, Chappell went on a field trip to a nearby wildlife refuge led by a wildlife biologist.

“We saw all these amazing birds,” Chappell said. “I still remember the American Avocet and the Black-necked stilt — looking at them through the scope. That was the moment I went from being a bird watcher, you might say, to being a birder.”

At age 15, he started leading Audubon field trips and Christmas Bird Counts in Washington state.

The National Audubon Society’s Christmas Bird Count started in 1900 and is the county’s longest running community science bird project. Since its mere beginnings over a century ago, thousands of volunteers across North and South America contribute to this wildlife census each year.

The Christmas Bird Count has enabled researchers and conservationists to study the long-term health and population trends of birds across the continent. Combined with other surveys such as the U.S. Geological Survey’s North American Breeding Bird Survey, started in 1966, the data has been vital for informing conservation strategies.

In 2002, the Cornell Lab of Ornithology launched its own participatory science program known as eBird. Since then, bird watchers worldwide have contributed to billions of bird observations that are compiled into a comprehensive database.  

Like the Christmas Bird Count, eBird has become an invaluable resource for conservation, helping scientists in better understanding bird abundance and distribution.

Without birders and hunters collecting data, many of the scientific discoveries in the bird world in the last half century might have been missed, including the documentation of widescale losses in North American bird populations.

Chappell — who is now 65-years-old and running his own bird watching tour business in Santa Fe, New Mexico — still regularly participates in Audubon’s Christmas Bird Count and is also recognized in the top 50 eBirders in the state.

Action Pt. 2 - ‘What’s good for the bird is good for the herd.’

While ranching and farming have contributed to the loss of grasslands, it is also a big part of the solution moving forward.

“You will hear from a lot of folks that work on grassland bird conservation that ranching is an essential part of conserving grassland birds,” Ryder said. “We have pithy statements like, ‘what’s good for the bird is good for the herd’.”

Historically, grassland birds evolved on the landscape with large ungulates such as bison. Cattle serve the same purpose in terms of the impact that they have on the vegetation and habitat for grassland birds.

Sarah Sortum, fourth generation owner of Switzer Ranch located in Burwell, Nebraska, began implementing bird conservation practices on her land about 15 years ago.  

In addition to their cattle grazing operations, Sortum and her family started a bird watching tourism business on the ranch in [TK]. Upon discovering the plight of grassland birds soon after, they started implementing conservation practices, such as prescribed burning and woody tree mitigation, in an effort to maintain the birds’ prairie habitat.

After a while, Sortum started to notice that improving the health of the ecosystem didn’t only benefit the birds, but it also benefited their cattle operation as well.

“When we realized that we could easily change a few management practices that not only would benefit the birds, but benefit our bottom line on our cattle operation as well, it was a no-brainer.”

Despite the Covid-19 Pandemic causing them to suspend bird watching tours, Sortum and her family continued on with their bird conservation practices.

“I really see the birds on our place as my barometer for our overall resource health,” Sortum said. “For ​​farmers and ranchers — like any other family business — your hope and dream is that generations after you will benefit from what you've done and will be able to continue with the same opportunities. If we don't have a healthy resource, then that's going to be very challenging for them.”

Switzer Ranch is one of many ranches and farms in the Great Plains that has implemented bird-friendly practices on their ranch.

Nick Schleissman, a bird monitoring technician who has worked more field jobs than he can remember, said compared to the spring and summer months during the breeding season, waking up at 5 a.m. is considered “sleeping in” for his line of work.

“I've always said as soon as my alarm is going off before 3 a.m., that's it — I'm not doing it,” Schleissman said.

The sun starts to break over the horizon as the Bird Conservancy caravan nears their destination, pulling off onto a dirt road in the middle of the desert. They drive slowly, watching and listening for birds that have just gotten a start to their morning.

Once they arrive at the site, the crew spends some time birding in order to assess where best to set up their mist nets. Their goal is to safely capture, tag and release the target species so that the collected data can be used for continued monitoring, which will aid in the species’ conservation.
